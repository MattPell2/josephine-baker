Josephine Baker

✦

01
St. Louis, Missouri · 1906

Born into Nothing

Josephine Baker in her early years as a performer

Josephine Baker was born Freda Josephine McDonald on June 3, 1906, in the Chestnut Valley neighborhood of St. Louis — a low-income district of rooming houses and railroad yards without indoor plumbing. Her mother, Carrie McDonald, was a washerwoman who had abandoned her own dreams of performing. Her father, Eddie Carson, a vaudeville drummer, abandoned the family shortly after Josephine's birth.

By the time she was eight, Josephine was working as a live-in domestic servant in white households. At eleven, she witnessed the East St. Louis Race Riots of 1917 — one of the worst episodes of racial violence in American history, leaving hundreds dead and thousands displaced. She never forgot it.

She was married for the first time at 13. Then again at approximately 15, to a railroad worker named Willie Baker, whose surname she kept for the rest of her life. She ran away from home, performed on the streets of St. Louis, and talked her way into a touring vaudeville troupe by quietly memorizing every role — then stepping in when an actress fell ill during a Boston run of Shuffle Along (1921), the landmark all-Black Broadway musical.

02
Paris · October 2, 1925

The Night Paris Fell in Love

In 1925, American socialite and producer Caroline Dudley Reagan was assembling a Black American jazz revue for Paris. She spotted Baker performing in New York, offered her $250 a week, and by September 1925 Baker was on a ship to France. She was nineteen years old and had never left the United States.

On October 2, 1925, Baker took the stage at the Théâtre des Champs-Élysées in La Revue Nègre and performed La Danse Sauvage wearing little more than feathers. Paris erupted. Picasso, Hemingway, F. Scott Fitzgerald, and the leading intellectuals of the era were in the audience and in the press coverage that followed. She was signed to headline the legendary Folies Bergère before the curtain fell that night.

The following year, 1926, she debuted her most iconic look: a girdle of sixteen drooping artificial bananas at the Folies Bergère — accompanied by her pet cheetah, Chiquita, on a diamond leash. The image became one of the most reproduced in 20th-century entertainment history. The French press gave her four nicknames: La Vénus Noire, La Perle Noire, La Vénus de Bronze, and La Déesse Créole.

The banana skirt, Folies Bergère — the most reproduced image in 20th-century entertainment
La Vénus Noire — Paris made her a global icon overnight

"In America, I had to go to the back door. In Paris, I walked through the front — and they gave me the whole building."

— Josephine Baker

In 1936 she returned briefly to the United States to appear in the Ziegfeld Follies. American critics were cruel in ways that their Parisian counterparts never were. She returned to France heartbroken — and became a French citizen in 1937. She would spend the rest of her life there.

03
The Silver Screen · 1927 onward

Barriers Broken

In 1927, Baker became the first Black woman to star in a major motion picture — the French silent film Siren of the Tropics, directed by Mario Nalpas and Henri Étiévant. Hollywood had no interest in giving a Black woman a starring role; France didn't require permission. The film ran for months to strong reviews across Europe.

French Citizenship · 1937
Renounced her American citizenship and formally made France her home. France gave her what America refused.

Her subsequent films — Zou Zou (1934, her first sound film) and Princesse Tam-Tam (1935) — were hits across Europe. She also composed and recorded music throughout the 1930s. Her signature song, J'ai Deux Amours ("I Have Two Loves"), released in 1930, became her personal anthem — referring publicly to France and America, and privately to something deeper.

04
World War II · 1939–1945

The Most Famous Spy in Paris

In September 1939, days after France declared war on Germany, Baker was recruited by Jacques Abtey, head of French counter-military intelligence (the Deuxième Bureau). He needed civilians with access to the highest social circles — diplomats, generals, Axis officials. Baker fit the profile perfectly.

She attended glamorous parties thrown by German, Italian, and Japanese officials in Paris, eavesdropping on conversations and extracting military intelligence while everyone asked for her autograph. She then carried classified information written in invisible ink on her sheet music, smuggled across international borders in her lingerie, pinned to her underwear, written on her hands. Border guards fawned over her at every crossing — they never searched her. She smuggled over 50 classified documents this way.

Baker during the WWII era — celebrated performer by day, intelligence operative by night

"Who would dare strip-search Josephine Baker?"

— Baker to her intelligence handler Jacques Abtey. She was right every single time.

Intelligence Baker gathered about Axis troop positions, coastal geography, and beach defenses in North Africa helped support Operation Torch — the Allied invasion of North Africa in November 1942. In May 1944, she was formally commissioned as a Sub-Lieutenant in the French Women's Auxiliary Air Force — making her a genuine military officer, not just a supporter.

05
Civil Rights · 1950s–1968

She Never Bent the Knee

Baker was one of the first major entertainers to take a hard public stand against segregation — decades before it became culturally safe to do so. Her contract with every American venue included a non-negotiable clause: integrated audiences only. When clubs pushed back, she walked. More often than not, they eventually caved.

The Stork Club · 1951

Baker was refused service at New York's most fashionable nightclub. She filed a public complaint and named the club openly as a racist establishment. A young Grace Kelly was sitting at the next table. She immediately got up and left in solidarity — beginning a friendship that would span the rest of their lives.

NAACP Day · 1951

The NAACP was so moved by Baker's refusal to perform for segregated crowds — and her willingness to say so publicly — that they declared May 20, 1951, "Josephine Baker Day" in her honor.

Denied Re-Entry · 1950s

Due to her civil rights activism and alleged "communist sympathies" (McCarthyism), Baker was denied a U.S. visa on at least two occasions in the 1950s. One of the most famous women in the world was banned from the country of her birth for speaking out against racism.

Offered the Movement · 1968

After Martin Luther King Jr.'s assassination, Coretta Scott King traveled personally to the Netherlands to ask Baker to assume MLK's role as leader of the Civil Rights Movement. Baker considered it for several days — then declined, saying her 12 children "were too young to lose their mother." The dangers were real.

06
August 28, 1963 · Washington D.C.

250,000 People. One Woman on Stage.

At age 57, Baker flew from France to Washington specifically for the March. She was the only official female speaker at the March on Washington — a fact that drew criticism from women's rights advocates, since all other women were acknowledged but not given speaking slots. Baker addressed the crowd of 250,000 immediately before Dr. King delivered "I Have a Dream."

She wore her French Resistance uniform — khaki jacket, military medals pinned across her chest. It was a deliberate statement: I served this cause before it had a name. I put my life on the line. I am not just a celebrity showing up.

"I have walked into the palaces of kings and queens and into the houses of presidents. And much more. But I could not walk into a hotel in America and get a cup of coffee, and that made me mad."

— Josephine Baker, March on Washington, August 28, 1963

She also told the crowd: "I could go into any restaurant I wanted to, and I could drink water anyplace I wanted to, and I didn't have to go to a colored toilet either, and I have to tell you it was nice, and I got used to it, and I liked it, and I wasn't afraid anymore that someone would shout at me." The crowd fell silent. Then roared.

07
Les Milandes Château · The Dordogne, France

The Rainbow Tribe

Baker conceived the Rainbow Tribe as a living experiment in human brotherhood. Rather than argue that people of different races, religions, and nationalities could coexist — she would simply prove it, in the most personal way imaginable. She purchased a 15th-century château called Les Milandes in the Dordogne valley of southwest France, opened it to paying tourists, and adopted twelve children from twelve countries, raised together under one roof.

Each child was raised in their own religious tradition — Catholic, Protestant, Jewish, Muslim — as a deliberate statement that all faiths could share a home. The world watched, sometimes in admiration and sometimes in bewilderment, as Baker turned her personal life into a public argument against prejudice.

08
1969 · 1975

The Fall. Then the Curtain Call.

By the late 1960s, Baker's boundless generosity — combined with contractors who repeatedly defrauded her — had led to catastrophic debt. Les Milandes was sold at auction for a fraction of its value. In 1969, photographers captured Baker sitting alone on the château's front steps after her eviction, surrounded by her belongings. The image went around the world and shocked it.

Her old friend Grace Kelly — now Princess Grace of Monaco — had never forgotten the woman who first befriended her at the Stork Club in 1951. She arranged for Baker and all twelve children to be given a villa on the French Riviera in Roquebrune-Cap-Martin. Baker was not homeless. She was not alone.

On April 8, 1975, Josephine Baker opened a grand comeback show at the Bobino Theater in Paris, tracing fifty years of her career in a single night. She was 68. The reviews were ecstatic. Paris gave her a standing ovation that felt like the entire city exhaling at once.

Baker in later life — still commanding every room she entered

Four days after the triumph — on April 12, 1975 — Baker was found unresponsive in her Paris apartment. She died of a cerebral hemorrhage. She was 68 years old.

France gave her a state funeral — the first American-born woman in French history to receive one. 20,000 people lined the streets of Paris.
09
November 30, 2021

Inducted into the Panthéon

The Panthéon is France's national mausoleum — the resting place of its greatest heroes: Voltaire, Rousseau, Victor Hugo, Marie Curie. On November 30, 2021, French President Emmanuel Macron presided over the induction of its newest honoree. Soldiers carried her cenotaph draped in the French tricolor up the steps along a red carpet as the military band played.

  • First Black woman ever admitted to the Panthéon
  • First American (of any race) ever admitted to the Panthéon
  • First performing artist ever admitted to the Panthéon
  • The sixth woman admitted in the institution's entire history
  • Her symbolic coffin holds soil from three places: St. Louis (her birthplace), Paris (her adopted home), and Monaco (where she is buried — per her family's wishes, her remains will stay there)

"A war heroine, fighter, dancer, singer — a Black person defending Black people, but first and foremost a woman defending humans."

— President Emmanuel Macron, Panthéon induction ceremony, November 30, 2021
10
The Details That Make Her Real

Things Worth Knowing

The Pet Cheetah

Baker owned a cheetah named Chiquita who wore a diamond collar, traveled the world with her, slept in her bed, and regularly appeared on stage at the Folies Bergère — escaping into the orchestra pit and terrorizing the musicians, which only made the show more legendary.

She Also Had a Pig Named Albert

At various points Baker also kept a chimpanzee, a snake, a goat who lived in her nightclub dressing room, and a pig named Albert who grew so large from eating in the kitchen that the kitchen doorframe had to be demolished to get him out.

1,500+ Marriage Proposals

Baker was married four times — twice before she was legally an adult — and is estimated to have received over 1,500 marriage proposals in her lifetime. She reportedly found most of them amusing.

The Timing of Her Death

She died four days after the greatest critical triumph of her late career. She had just proven, at 68, that she could still command a stage completely. The timing gives her story the quality of myth: she went out on top, at full volume, with Paris on its feet.
