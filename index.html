A Life Without Parallel
Josephine Baker
June 3, 1906 — April 12, 1975
Performer Spy Civil Rights Icon Mother of 12 French National Hero
St. Louis, Missouri · 1906

Born into
Nothing

  • Born Freda Josephine McDonald to a single mother who washed laundry for white families
  • No indoor plumbing. Grew up in a rooming-house neighborhood near the railroad yards
  • Sent to work as a live-in maid for white families at age 8
  • Married at 13 — then again at 15, keeping her second husband's name: Baker
  • At age 11, she witnessed the East St. Louis Race Riots — one of the worst racial massacres in U.S. history
  • Ran away from home at 13. Performed on the streets to survive
  • Talked her way into a touring vaudeville troupe by memorizing every role — without being asked
  • By 19, she was on a ship to Paris. She never looked back.
Paris · October 2, 1925

The Night Paris
Fell in Love

At 19, Josephine Baker stepped onto the stage of the Théâtre des Champs-Élysées and performed La Danse Sauvage. Paris went electric. She was signed to headline the legendary Folies Bergère before the curtain fell.

Barriers

In 1927, Baker became the first Black woman to star in a major motion picture — the French silent film Siren of the Tropics. Hollywood wouldn't allow it. Paris didn't need permission.

World War II · 1939–1945

The Most Famous
Spy in Paris

Recruited by French intelligence in 1939, Baker used her celebrity as a cover. She attended Axis diplomatic parties, eavesdropped on generals, and smuggled classified intelligence written in invisible ink on her sheet music — across international borders that no guard dared fully search.

Civil Rights · 1950s – 1968

She Never
Bent the Knee

  • Refused to perform for any segregated audience in America — ever. When venues pushed back, she fought until they desegregated.
  • The Stork Club, 1951: Refused service in NYC's most fashionable club. Filed a public complaint. A young Grace Kelly walked out in solidarity — beginning a lifelong friendship.
  • The NAACP declared May 20, 1951, "Josephine Baker Day."
  • Denied a U.S. visa — twice — in the 1950s for her activism and alleged communist sympathies during McCarthyism
  • In 1968, Coretta Scott King personally asked Baker to take over as leader of the Civil Rights Movement after MLK's assassination. Baker considered it for days — then declined, saying her 12 children were too young to lose their mother.
August 28, 1963

250,000 People.
One Woman on Stage.

Baker was the only official female speaker at the March on Washington. She stood at the microphone in her French Resistance uniform — khaki jacket, military medals pinned to her chest — and spoke immediately before Dr. King delivered "I Have a Dream."

"I have walked into the palaces of kings and queens and into the houses of presidents. And much more. But I could not walk into a hotel in America and get a cup of coffee, and that made me mad."

Les Milandes Château · The Dordogne, France

The Rainbow Tribe

Baker adopted 12 children from 12 different countries and religions — and raised them together in a 15th-century French château she opened to tourists, to prove that people of every race and faith could live as one family.

1969 · 1975

The Fall.
Then the Curtain Call.

The Fall — 1969
  • Debt and fraud stripped Baker of Les Milandes. She was photographed sitting on the château steps after eviction — a photo that stunned the world.
  • Her childhood friend Grace Kelly — now Princess Grace of Monaco — gave Baker and her 12 children a villa on the French Riviera.
  • A decade of hardship followed.
The Comeback — April 8, 1975
  • At 68 years old, Baker opened a grand comeback show at the Bobino Theater in Paris — tracing 50 years of her career in one night.
  • Standing ovations. Critics called it the greatest performance of her life. Paris wept.
  • Four days later, she was found unresponsive in her apartment. She died on April 12, 1975, of a cerebral hemorrhage.
  • France gave her a state funeral — the first American-born woman to receive one. 20,000 people lined the streets of Paris.
November 30, 2021

Inducted into
The Panthéon

  • First Black woman ever admitted to France's Panthéon
  • First American ever admitted to the Panthéon
  • First performing artist ever admitted to the Panthéon
  • The sixth woman admitted — among Voltaire, Rousseau, Victor Hugo, Marie Curie
  • Soldiers carried her cenotaph up the steps draped in the French tricolor. Soil inside from St. Louis, Paris, and Monaco.

    • President Macron: "A war heroine, fighter, dancer, singer — devoted to our ideals."

    Women's History Month

    What She Left
    Behind

    On Stage

    One of the most celebrated performers in history. Helped define the Jazz Age. First Black headliner at the Folies Bergère. First Black woman in a major film.

    In War

    A genuine decorated intelligence operative — not just a supporter. Légion d'Honneur. Croix de Guerre. Formally commissioned as a military officer.

    For Civil Rights

    Refused to perform for segregated crowds decades before the movement. Only official female speaker at the March on Washington. Turned down leadership of the movement to protect her children.

    As a Mother

    Proved — with 12 children from 12 countries — that race, religion, and nationality are no barrier to a family. A living refutation of prejudice.

    "Surely the day will come when color means nothing more than skin tone, when religion is seen uniquely as a way to speak one's soul; when birth places have the weight of a throw of the dice and all men are born free, when understanding breeds love and brotherhood."
    — Josephine Baker
